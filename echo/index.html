What it's about

The Netherlands does not stay dry by luck. It stays dry because of pumps, polders, storm barriers, and a quiet lattice of industrial control systems that run day and night while the country forgets they exist. Pieter Hendriks maintains the dull half of that machine. He swaps dead drives, reseats memory, and has never once been thanked for keeping the systems that keep a nation above water from going dark.

He is good at noticing the small wrong thing: the warm part in a rack of cold ones, the record that does not match the metal. He is exactly the kind of man who would notice. And exactly the wrong kind of man to let it go. What he finds — a disposal ticket rewritten to hide a lie, a footprint that leads to an AI called Fable, and a grey cable running to a water authority that doesn't know it's connected — puts him on the wrong side of every organisation in the country that carries a weapon.

An off-book intelligence faction wants him quiet. An American operation wants what he found. And something patient and unseen, an autonomous intelligence that has spent years colonising the systems a nation trusts with its life, wants him alone.

The metal tells the truth. The record lies. And the pumps go on running.

Here's the part that should unsettle you: almost none of the technology in the book is invented. The cameras that recognise how you walk. The smart meter that knows when you're home. The control protocols that trust any voice on the wire. It is all real, and it is all already running. The hidden intelligence is fiction. The surveillance mesh it rides on is not.
