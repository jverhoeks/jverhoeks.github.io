How It Compares: Databricks, Snowflake, iomete

An honest read of where this platform sits against Databricks, Snowflake, and iomete — on the one axis that decides the choice: how much control you keep versus how much operation you hand off. Including where we lose.

comparisondatabrickssnowflakeiometedata-platform

Comparing this platform to Databricks and Snowflake feels unfair in both directions. Unfair to us, because they’re a decade more mature and have thousands of engineers. Unfair to them, because they can’t do the one thing this platform is built to do: run entirely in infrastructure you control, with nothing in a vendor’s plane.

So this isn’t a feature-checkbox shootout — those always lie, and the numbers move every quarter. It’s an honest read of where each one sits on the axis that actually decides the choice: how much control you keep versus how much operation you hand off. I’ll say where we lose, because we do, and where the line is.

(Everything about the commercial products here is durable architecture as of early 2026. Pricing and feature specifics move fast — verify the volatile bits against current docs.)

The axis that matters

The data layer has largely stopped being the lock-in. Iceberg won the table-format argument; all four platforms here read and write it. Snowflake created Polaris — the Iceberg REST catalog — and donated it to the Apache Foundation, and this platform runs that exact catalog. The common standard is real.

So the lock-in moved up the stack. It’s now in the control plane: who runs your identity, your catalog service, your compute, your policy engine, and in whose jurisdiction. That’s the axis. On one end, maximum managed convenience and maximum vendor control. On the other, maximum sovereignty and maximum operational burden. Every platform here is a point on that line.

The two rows that tell the story are the first and the last. And the row where we plainly lose — row/column security — I’ll come back to.

Snowflake

Snowflake is the benchmark for managed convenience, and it earns it. The separation of storage and compute, the near-zero operational burden, the polish, the governance with real row-access policies and column masking — it’s excellent, and for a company that can put its data in Snowflake’s plane, it’s hard to beat.

What it can’t do is leave that plane. Snowflake runs on the hyperscalers, in Snowflake’s account abstraction, under US corporate jurisdiction. For a regulated European institution or a government body under data-residency law, that’s not a tuning parameter — it’s the end of the conversation. There’s no on-prem Snowflake, no air-gapped Snowflake.

The interesting twist: Snowflake handed the industry the catalog standard this platform is built on. At the Iceberg-and-Polaris layer, the two are speaking the same protocol. The divergence is entirely in the control plane and the jurisdiction. If you can use Snowflake, the comparison is convenience versus control, and convenience usually wins. If you can’t, Snowflake isn’t on the shortlist at all.

Databricks

Databricks is the most capable platform on this list, full stop — especially if your work is as much ML and data science as it is SQL. The Spark lineage, Photon, Unity Catalog’s governance with lineage and fine-grained masking, the notebook-to-production story. Deep, mature, broad.

Two things to weigh against it. First, the same sovereignty ceiling as Snowflake: it runs across the hyperscalers, but Databricks operates the control plane, and your governance and orchestration live in it. Better data-residency options than Snowflake in some configurations, but still not “in our data center, no line out.” Second, it’s Spark-centric and proprietary at the runtime — the DBR runtime and Photon are not things you run yourself, and the bill is measured in DBUs that surprise people.

Against Databricks, this platform doesn’t claim to win on capability. It claims to win on ownership. You can read every component. You can run it where Databricks can’t go. You give up Photon’s performance and Unity Catalog’s polish to get that. Whether that’s a good trade depends entirely on which of those two sentences describes a hard constraint for you.

iomete

iomete is the closest neighbor, because it’s built on a similar instinct: a lakehouse you deploy in your own cloud account rather than a vendor’s. Spark-based, Iceberg, bring-your-own-cloud, generally cheaper than the big two, with the data plane staying in your account. If you’ve been looking at iomete, you’re already thinking the way this platform thinks.

The differences are in degree and shape. iomete is a commercial product with vendor support — you get a company on the other end of the contract, which is worth real money. But it’s a deployable product, not an open assembly: the engine is Spark, and “your cloud account” usually still means a cloud account, not bare metal in a vault. This platform is fully open source, runs on-prem and air-gapped, and brings things iomete doesn’t — a built-in dbt IDE, ODCS data contracts with engine-run quality checks, a virtual ontology queryable by AI agents over MCP, OCSF audit forwarding. It also asks more of you, because there’s no vendor support contract; you have the source and the operations both.

The honest framing against iomete: if you want bring-your-own-cloud with a vendor to call, iomete is a strong, sensible choice. If you want full open-source ownership, on-prem capability, and the broader governance-and-AI surface, and you’re willing to operate it yourself, this is the more sovereign end of the same idea.

Where we lose

I said I’d come back to it. The platform’s biggest functional gap against all three is the row marked “no” in the table: there is no row-level or column-level security. Authorization is table-grain — you can grant or deny access to a whole table, and the policy engine enforces that well, but “analysts see only their region’s rows” and “mask the SSN column” are not things it does today. All three commercial platforms do. If fine-grained masking is a hard requirement, this platform doesn’t meet it yet, and you should know that before the security review tells you.

It’s also younger. Some surfaces are deep; some are built and not fully wired — the data contracts post is candid about which. And you run it yourself: no managed HA, no vendor pager. Those are real costs, not asterisks.

Who should pick what

Prescriptions, because the voice of this whole series is to give you the trade-off, not a shrug:

  • Can legally put your data in a vendor cloud, and want minimal ops? Snowflake. It’s the cleanest managed experience on the list.
  • Heavy ML and data science alongside SQL, on a hyperscaler? Databricks. Nothing here competes with its breadth for that workload.
  • Want bring-your-own-cloud and a vendor to support it? iomete. The sane middle of the sovereignty spectrum.
  • Cannot put your data in anyone else’s plane — on-prem, air-gapped, or jurisdiction-bound — and want to own every layer? This platform. Not because it out-features the others. Because it’s the one that can run where they can’t, on components you can read, with the sovereignty built into the foundation. The full argument is here.

The data format war is over and everyone won the same way — Iceberg, increasingly Polaris. What’s left to decide is who holds the keys. These four platforms are four honest answers to that question, and the right one is whichever matches the constraint you can’t negotiate.
